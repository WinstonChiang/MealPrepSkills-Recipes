Well picked produce will have more flavor, more yield, better nutrients, and save money.
Picking produce follows some simple guidelines, but can become much more specific for various goods.
At the very baseline, touch and inspect your fruits and vegetables before buying for any signs of mold or discoloration.
Density can be a good indicator; the more dense the more juice.
Seasonality is an excellent starting point, even if many grocery stories are stocked with the essentials year-round, seasonal items (gourds in fall/winter, young greens in spring), will be the freshest and often cheaper.

Picking good produce is vital to making good food and to save money by limiting waste. As experience grows, finding the best fruit in a bin becomes easy and fun.

onions, 
![I and My friends]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/img/we-in-rest.jpg)
There are three general types of onions that are nearly identically interchangable. 

Yellow, classic, basic onion. Sweat and saute or caramelize
White, useful in raw or lightly pickled applications, think salsa or guacamole or on burgers.
Red Onions, useful for pickling, garnish, sauteed

Other Alliums include:
Scallion or Green Onions
Shallots
Leeks
Chives

Shopping Produce Guide: Denser is better. Check for blemishes that are more than skin deep. Check for softness or rot on tops or root-bottoms as indicators for interior layers.  Deeply wrinkled onions are old and not good.


peppers, 
![I and My friends]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/img/we-in-rest.jpg)
Bell Peppers come in Green, Yellow, Orange, Red, which is also an indication of how long they were able to ripen on the vine. Red will be the sweetest. Greens are better in cooked applications.

Shopping Produce Guide: Smooth skin, no blemishes. Wrinkled skin indicates old produce.
